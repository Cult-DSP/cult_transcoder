Introduction
Next-generation audio formats enable immersive spatial audio experiences by moving beyond traditional stereo or 5.1 channels. Modern standards can represent sound scenes with channel-based, object-based, and scene-based (ambisonics) audio elements to achieve 3D audio immersion 1 2 . This design document outlines a plan to support key open standards – MPEG-H 3D Audio, Immersive Audio Model & Format (IAMF), and Higher-Order Ambisonics (HOA) – in a cross-platform C++ tool for parsing and authoring immersive audio content. We focus on open-source implementations (permissive licensing) to allow integration into an open-source workflow, and we prioritize portability (macOS initially, with Windows/ Linux support) by avoiding any platform-specific dependencies. The tool’s scope will be primarily data parsing and file authoring, i.e. reading and writing these immersive audio formats (while leaving advanced signal processing or rendering to external modules). All design decisions and technical facts in this document are backed by literature and specifications, to serve as a reliable reference for future development and academic writing.
Background: Immersive Audio Standards and Models MPEG-H 3D Audio (ISO/IEC 23008-3)
MPEG-H 3D Audio is an international standard for next-generation audio, finalized in 2015 as part of the MPEG-H suite 3 . It was designed to deliver immersive and interactive audio across use cases “ranging from highest-quality cable and satellite TV down to streaming to mobile devices” 4 . Unlike legacy formats, MPEG-H can carry a mix of channels, audio objects, and ambisonic sound fields in one bitstream 5 . In practice, this means a single MPEG-H audio stream might include conventional speaker channels (e.g. a 5.1 bed), dynamic sound objects with positional metadata (for dialogue or effects), and scene-based ambisonics for ambience 5 2 . The standard supports up to 128 coded channels and 64 rendered loudspeaker outputs, providing a superset of Dolby Atmos capabilities 6 .
Adoption: MPEG-H 3D Audio is one of the first open, standardized alternatives to proprietary formats like Dolby Atmos 6 . It has been adopted in broadcast and streaming applications – for example, the MPEG-H Audio system was selected for the ATSC 3.0 next-gen TV broadcast standard in certain regions 7 . South Korea’s UHDTV broadcasts and some European trials use MPEG-H for immersive audio, while other regions use Dolby AC-4; both MPEG-H and AC-4 are recognized in ATSC 3.0. This demonstrates the format’s maturity and interoperability in real-world delivery systems.
Technical Features: MPEG-H’s codec architecture builds on MPEG-USAC (Unified Speech and Audio Coding) for waveform compression, with extensions for 3D audio signals 8 9 . On decoding, a flexible renderer adapts content to the playback setup: channel-based elements are downmixed or upmixed to the speaker layout, object-based elements are rendered via positional metadata (using techniques like VBAP – Vector
1
Base Amplitude Panning 10 ), and HOA ambisonics are decoded to the target speakers via an ambisonic renderer 11 12 . This ensures “format agnostic” delivery – content can be authored once and rendered optimally on anything from a 22.2 theater system to a binaural headphone output 13 11 . The standard also defines comprehensive metadata for personalization (e.g. adjusting dialog level, switching language tracks) and loudness/dynamic-range control to meet broadcast requirements 14 15 . In short, MPEG-H provides a rich toolset for immersive and interactive audio, while remaining agnostic to the loudspeaker setup or listening device.
Immersive Audio Model & Format (IAMF)
IAMF (Immersive Audio Model & Format) is a much more recent open standard, released by the Alliance for Open Media (AOM) in 2024 16 17 . It is AOMedia’s first audio container specification, offered under a royalty-free license to encourage wide adoption 17 . IAMF is essentially an audio bitstream container and metadata model designed for the internet era, enabling easy mixing and playback of immersive audio across streaming, gaming, AR/VR, and broadcasting applications 18 19 . A major goal is to provide a license-free, codec-agnostic alternative to proprietary spatial audio formats (like Dolby Atmos) that can integrateseamlesslywithexistingdeliveryecosystems 20 21 .
Format and Capabilities: Technically, IAMF defines an audio bitstream format composed of self-contained OpenBitstreamUnits(OBUs)carryingaudiodataandmetadata 22 23 .Itiscodec-agnostic:audioframes within IAMF can be encoded in Opus, AAC-LC, FLAC, PCM, etc., as signaled by the bitstream 24 . This flexibility means IAMF isn’t tied to one particular compression codec; instead, it focuses on describing the spatial scene and coordinating decoder outputs. Similar to MPEG-H, IAMF supports traditional channels, discrete objects, and ambisonics: it can deliver “both channel-based and scene-based (ambisonics) spatial audio presentations” to a wide range of devices 25 . In fact, hybrid mixes are allowed – e.g. a 3.1.2 channel bed combined with a first-order ambisonic bed, or third-order Ambisonics paired with a stereo stream for non-diegetic music 26 . The format supports multiple mix presentations and mix configurations in one file, allowing different renditions (for instance, a theater mix vs. a headphone-optimized mix) or user- selectable options (like alternate languages or director’s commentary) within the same audio package 27 . Importantly, IAMF includes metadata to signal rendering instructions to the playback system – it can indicate which rendering algorithm to use, such as the EBU ADM Renderer (EAR) for loudspeaker output or the Binaural EBU ADM Renderer (BEAR) for headphone virtualization 28 29 . This ensures consistent results by using standardized renderers; in fact, the EAR Production Suite (an open-source toolkit compliant with ITU-R BS.2127) already provides loudspeaker and binaural renderers that align with IAMF’s guidelines

Open and Developer-Friendly: A key aspect of IAMF is its openness and focus on creators. As AOMedia’s Chairman noted, “IAMF represents a significant step forward in open immersive audio standards... as an open media project, it is easily accessible to implementers worldwide, fostering innovation” 32 . The specification v1.0 and subsequent updates are publicly available 16 , and AOMedia provides a reference software implementation. The libiamf reference library (currently focused on decoding) is open-sourced under a BSD-3-Clause license and available on AOM’s GitHub 33 34 . By late 2024, IAMF v1.1 was released with an updated reference decoder, and there are plans to add an official encoding tool for IAMF in the near future (to handle compressing mixes into the IAMF bitstream) 27 . Because IAMF is open, other tools have already integrated support: for example, FFmpeg has added demuxing and decoding for IAMF by incorporating libiamf, and Google’s Eclipsa Audio project provides higher-level tools and workflows for IAMF content creation 35 36 .

Google and Samsung spearheaded IAMF’s development and announced “Eclipsa Audio” in 2025 as a user-friendly open format based on IAMF 37 38 . Eclipsa Audio aims to “bring an immersive audio experience... for everyone” by making spatial audio creation accessible beyond Hollywood studios 37 . Starting in 2025, YouTube began supporting IAMF/Eclipsa Audio tracks for uploaded videos, marking the first major streaming platform adoption 39 . An industry certification program for devices is also in the works 40 . This rapid uptake underscores the importance of supporting IAMF in our tool – it is poised to become a widely used standard for immersive audio distribution in open ecosystems.
Higher-Order Ambisonics (Scene-Based Audio)
Ambisonics is a representation of a full spherical sound field, independent of any specific speaker layout. It falls under scene-based audio, using mathematical expansions of the sound field into spherical harmonics. MPEG-H and IAMF both embrace ambisonics as a key component for immersive scenes – referred to as HOA (Higher Order Ambisonics) in MPEG-H and similarly supported in IAMF 5 41 . In an HOA stream, instead of discrete speaker feeds or objects, the audio is encoded as a set of coefficient signals (Ambisonic channels) that capture the sound field up to a certain order 5 . These coefficients are “speaker-layout agnostic”, meaning they can be decoded to any output configuration given the appropriate renderer 42
5 . For example, a first-order Ambisonics (FOA) stream uses 4 channels (W, X, Y, Z) to represent omni and figure-8 components of the field; higher orders use more channels (e.g., 16 channels for third-order). The advantage is flexibility: one HOA recording can render to headphones (binaurally) or to various multichannel speaker rigs by mathematically “reconstructing” the sound field for that layout.
Modern implementations use the AmbiX convention for HOA: orthonormalized spherical harmonics with ACN channel ordering and SN3D normalization 43 . Both MPEG-H and IAMF adhere to this convention for interoperability. The IAMF spec explicitly adopts AmbiX (ACN/SN3D) as its format for any Ambisonics content
43 . Decoding ambisonics typically involves applying a decoding matrix (often derived from continuum decoding or energy-preserving decoding formulas) to map the HOA channels to the desired speaker feeds. IAMF specifies that ambisonics decoding and reconstruction should conform to IETF RFC 8486 when using Opus-coded HOA (this RFC defines the ambisonics carriage for Opus audio) 44 45 . In practice, existing libraries and tools (like Google’s VR Audio SDK or libspatialaudio) provide functions to rotate, decode, or binaurally render Ambisonic soundfields.
For our purposes, ambisonics will not be a separate format to parse, but rather a type of audio content that may be encountered inside MPEG-H or IAMF streams. The tool must recognize when a stream or audio element is HOA and handle its metadata accordingly. If converting between formats, it should preserve ambisonic order, channel ordering (ACN) and normalization (SN3D) correctly. As needed, we can leverage open libraries for Ambisonics processing (for example, libspatialaudio for FOA or the ambisonic decoders in the EBU EAR suite) when rendering or monitoring, but core library support might not be necessary just to mux/demux HOA content. Instead, correct interpretation of ambisonic metadata (e.g. channel count corresponds to an Ambisonic order via $N=(order+1)^2$ for ACN) and perhaps handling rotation metadata is key. Both target formats treat HOA as just another audio element: MPEG-H can apply a specialized HOA encoder/decoder in its chain 46 47 , and IAMF’s model includes an AmbisonicsConfig OBU to define the HOA setup within a stream 48 49 . In summary, supporting ambisonics means ensuring our tool can
3

carry through these HOA signals and metadata without degrading them, and possibly providing options to decode them externally if needed.
Audio Definition Model (ADM) and Object Metadata
While not an audio codec, the Audio Definition Model (ADM) deserves mention as an important standard for authoring and exchanging immersive audio scenes. ADM is an ITU-R specification (BS.2076) and an open metadata model adopted by EBU and others for describing channel, object, and HOA-based audio content in a structured way 50 . Essentially, ADM provides a standardized XML schema to define audio objects (with labels, positions versus time, gain, etc.), channel layouts, and scene descriptions in a production. ADM can be embedded in Broadcast Wave files (BW64) as a metadata chunk, creating an ADM- BWF which bundles audio tracks with their metadata. This model is format-agnostic and serves as a bridge between production tools and delivery formats. For instance, a mixing engineer might export an ADM file from a digital audio workstation; that ADM can then be used to produce a MPEG-H bitstream or an IAMF file (the metadata guides how to treat each audio stem). In fact, the open IAMF workflow explicitly supports ADM input: Google’s Eclipsa toolset allows ingesting an ADM BWF and converting it to an IAMF track 51 . ADM is also the basis of the EBU’s renderer (EAR/BEAR) which we mentioned – the renderers interpret ADM metadatatoproducethecorrectoutputforagivensetup 28 .
For our tool, ADM support is important for the “authoring” side. We want to allow users to import or export ADM files so that our tool can interface with other workflows and potentially convert between MPEG-H and IAMF while preserving object metadata. Rather than reinvent an ADM parser, we can utilize existing open- source libraries. The EBU’s libadm (Apache-2.0 licensed) is a C++ library specifically for parsing, creating, andwritingADMXMLdata 50 .IthandlestheITU-RBS.2076schemaandcanoutputADMXMLorevenhelp write ADM into a BW64 wave file (via integration with a BW64 library) 50 52 . By using libadm, we ensure compliance with the standard and minimize the risk of misinterpreting metadata. Our design will incorporate libadm to parse ADM files (or chunks) into an internal representation of audio objects and scene data. Conversely, if we need to output an ADM (for instance, to allow an export of an IAMF or MPEG-H scene back to ADM for editing), libadm can be used to construct the XML from our internal data model.
ADM will effectively act as the common interchange format in many cases. For example, a possible workflow is: decode an MPEG-H bitstream into individual audio tracks and ADM metadata (MPEG-H carries metadata known as MHAS/MAE which can be mapped to ADM concepts 14 15 ), then encode those tracks and metadata into an IAMF file. Having ADM as an intermediate ensures the object positions, gain structures, and intent are preserved across the conversion. Therefore, robust ADM handling is a cornerstone of the tool’s authoring capabilities.
Implementation Considerations and Libraries Choice of Programming Language: C++
We will implement the tool in C++ for performance, portability, and library compatibility. Immersive audio processing can be computationally heavy (especially if real-time encoding or decoding is involved), and C++ allows fine-grained performance tuning when needed (e.g., using vectorization libraries for DSP). More importantly, many of the available open-source libraries for these formats are written in C or C++ (e.g., Fraunhofer’s MPEG-H decoder, AOM’s libiamf in C99, EBU’s libadm in C++11). Using C++ enables us to integrate those libraries natively. In contrast, Python implementations (such as some of the EBU tools) are
4

not ideal for our use-case, as we require cross-platform compiled binaries and likely need to embed these capabilities in other software or real-time systems. We prefer to avoid any Python dependencies unless absolutely necessary – and current evidence suggests none of the core functionality requires Python. For example, the EBU ADM Renderer has a Python version, but a C++ version (libear/libadm) exists which we will use 53 . By sticking to C++, we ensure macOS, Windows, and Linux can be targeted with one codebase, and we can produce command-line tools or library modules suitable for each OS. C++ is also well-suited for an open-source project, with broad developer familiarity and no runtime prerequisites beyond the standard libraries, which aligns with our distribution goals.

This decoder comes with a permissive-but-specialized license (the FDK license) similar to the one for Fraunhofer’s FDK AAC codec 57 . If needed, the library’s packager can help author new bitstreams (though Fraunhofer’s MPEG-H Authoring Suite is a separate set of tools not fully open-sourced, we can still output raw MHAS streams or use FFmpeg for MP4 multiplexing).

Integrating Ambisonics Support
As discussed, handling Ambisonics is largely about carrying the HOA data through the pipelines. If we need to decode ambisonics to multi-channel audio or binaural, we might integrate an existing renderer. One promising library is the EBU’s libear, a C++14 library implementing the core of the EAR (ADM Renderer) for loudspeakers 53 . It can render object and HOA content to arbitrary layouts according to ITU BS.2127. The companion libvisr (used in BEAR) provides binaural rendering with HRTFs. However, adding full rendering might be beyond the scope of “parsing and authoring,” and the user specifically noted “we should be able to handle the rest ourselves,” which likely refers to custom rendering or playback outside this tool. Therefore, our implementation will treat ambisonic content essentially as a special audio track type. We will ensure our internal model flags when a track is Ambisonics (with its order, normalization, etc.), and we will preserve all necessary metadata. For example, in ADM, an audioTrack can be labeled as HOA with order N; in MPEG-H, HOAcontentcomeswithmetadata(likeanindicationofpredominantvsambientcomponents 47 78 ),and in IAMF the AmbisonicsConfig OBU specifies how multiple substreams form the HOA set 48 49 . Internal Data Model and Conversion Logic
To enable authoring (conversion from one format to another), we will design an internal representation of an immersive audio scene. This internal model will likely resemble ADM since ADM is the most generalized: it will have objects with attributes (position, gain, etc.), channels (with speaker labels or polar coordinates if needed), and ambisonic scene descriptions (order, etc.). Essentially, we want a structure that can losslessly represent what’s in an MPEG-H file or an IAMF file. ADM’s schema can be our guide (with entities like audioProgramme, audioObject, audioChannel, audioPack, audioStream, etc.). When parsing an MPEG-H stream, we will populate this internal model: e.g., create an audioObject for each MPEG-H object (using its metadata for position and content), create channel sets for channel-based elements, and represent HOA as a special audioObject or as audioPack of channels with HOA flag. Similarly, when parsing IAMF, we’ll map IAMF’s AudioElements and MixPresentations to our internal structure. Once in the internal form, we can then emit to the target format by reversing the mapping: if writing IAMF, construct the OBUs from our internal data; if writing MPEG-H, feed the internal data to an encoder library.
One design decision is how to handle audio codecs: Since MPEG-H uses its own codec (USAC-based) and IAMF might use Opus/AAC, converting between them means re-encoding audio. Our tool will need to invoke encoders/decoders appropriately. For decoding: Fraunhofer’s libmpeghdec and AOM’s libiamf will give us PCM audio for each element. For encoding: Ittiam’s MPEG-H encoder will encode PCM to MPEG-H frames; for IAMF, we may need to encode PCM to Opus/AAC as part of creating OBUs (we can use libOpus or libFDK-AAC for that). All these encoders have suitable open licenses (Opus is BSD, FDK-AAC is again that FDK license if we choose it). The architecture might involve a pipeline like: PCM Audio -> Opus Encoder -> OBU packer for IAMF, or PCM -> MPEG-H Encoder (which internally does the perceptual encoding and multiplexer) for MPEG-H.
Crucially, the open-source license compliance will be maintained. We will include only libraries with permissive licenses or those that allow linking in open-source (FDK’s license, while not Apache/MIT, is acceptable as a standalone component and widely distributed – e.g., Android includes FDK AAC). libmpeghdec’s FDK license will need us to include its text and possibly avoid GPL combination (but our project can be Apache or BSD and include FDK-licensed code as a separate module). Ittiam’s BSD-3 libraries pose no issue. libadm is Apache-2.0, perfect for integration 79 . libiamf is BSD. Thus, the final tool can be released under an OSI-approved license (likely Apache-2.0 or MIT) while satisfying all dependencies’ requirements. We will document which third-party code is included and ensure attribution as required.
Cross-Platform and OS Considerations
To reiterate, all chosen components are cross-platform and do not rely on OS-specific audio APIs. We explicitly avoid technologies like Apple’s CoreAudio 3D Audio or Windows Sonic SDK – those are platform- tied and not needed since we handle spatial audio at the format level. The libraries (Fraunhofer’s, Ittiam’s, AOM’s, EBU’s) use standard C/C++ and have been built on Mac, Windows, Linux (some even provide Xcode or Visual Studio project files or CMake presets for each) 56 55 . Our build system will use CMake to orchestrate these dependencies, which eases multiplatform building. For example, libadm can be installed via Homebrew on Mac or built from source easily 80 ; Fraunhofer’s decoder uses CMake and supports
8

VS2019 on Windows out of the box 56 . We will include continuous integration tests on all three desktop OSes to verify functionality.
One thing to consider is endianess and alignment – since we might deal with binary data (like reading little-endian WAV files or bitstreams) – but all target platforms are little-endian nowadays, and libraries handle byte-order where needed. We will also ensure that file path handling, threading, etc., use cross- platform practices (e.g., avoid assumptions about file separators or available APIs). If any library lacks Windows support, we will find alternatives. But given the information: Fraunhofer explicitly lists Windows support 81 , libiamf being a reference likely was tested on Linux and Windows (and we can compile on Windows), libadm works on Windows (via Visual Studio or MinGW) as it’s pure C++11.
In summary, the design chooses components and approaches that are inherently portable. There is no dependency that is macOS-only or Linux-only. By avoiding such traps, we ensure the tool can later be built for Windows and potentially packaged as a plugin or binary for that platform with minimal friction.
Conclusion and Next Steps
By leveraging these open-source libraries and standards, we can create a comprehensive toolset for immersive audio parsing and authoring. The combination of MPEG-H 3D Audio (the broadcast-grade, object-based audio codec) and IAMF (the new internet-friendly spatial audio format) covers both traditional and emerging use cases for 3D audio. Incorporating Ambisonics support and the Audio Definition Model ensures that our tool can serve as a bridge between content creation and distribution – allowing conversion and interchange between formats while preserving spatial intent. We will implement the core in C++ for efficiency and integrate permissively-licensed libraries (Fraunhofer FDK MPEG-H, Ittiam’s encoders, AOM’s libiamf, EBU’s libadm, etc.) so that the end result is fully open source and ready for cross-platform deployment. This design, with its citations from standards and research, not only guides development but also provides a foundation for scholarly reference. As immersive audio continues to grow (with consumer expectations “on the rise” for personalization and 3D sound 82 ), an open tool like this will be invaluable for developers and researchers alike to experiment, convert, and deliver next-gen audio experiences without being locked into proprietary ecosystems. All the pieces are now in place to proceed with implementation, starting with wrapping the decoder libraries and building the internal scene representation, then adding encoding capabilities and extensive validation with real-world content. With this document as a reference, we can ensure each implementation choice is justified by industry practice or standard, paving the way for a robust and credible software tool.
